## Undervalued Company Identification Criteria

This standard defines the quantitative criteria for identifying potentially undervalued companies. These metrics serve as a screening mechanism and should be followed by qualitative analysis and further due diligence.

### Core Financial Ratios and Thresholds

| Metric | Condition | Rationale |
| :-------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio** | `P/E > 0` (positive earnings) <br> AND <br> `P/E < 15.0` (absolute threshold) <br> OR <br> `P/E < Industry Average PE` (relative to peers) | A low P/E ratio suggests that the market is undervaluing the company's earnings power. Comparing to an industry average provides context, while an absolute threshold screens for exceptionally low valuations. Exclude negative P/E due to losses. |
| **Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio** | `P/B > 0` <br> AND <br> `P/B < 1.5` (absolute threshold) <br> OR <br> `P/B < Industry Average PB` | A P/B ratio less than 1.5 (or below industry average) can indicate that the company's stock is trading for less than the book value of its assets, suggesting undervaluation. Exclude negative P/B if book value is negative, which usually indicates severe financial distress. |
| **Return on Equity (ROE)** | `ROE > 10.0%` (meaning the company is generating at least 10 cents of profit for every dollar of shareholder equity) | A strong ROE indicates efficient use of shareholder investments to generate profit, which is a hallmark of a healthy and potentially undervalued company when combined with low valuation ratios. |
| **Debt-to-Equity (D/E) Ratio** | `D/E < 1.0` (meaning total debt is less than shareholder equity) | A low D/E ratio signifies that a company is less reliant on debt financing and has a healthier financial structure, reducing risk and potentially making it more attractive to value investors. |
| **Current Ratio** | `Current Ratio > 1.5` (meaning current assets can cover current liabilities 1.5 times over) | A current ratio above 1.5 indicates good short-term liquidity, suggesting the company can easily meet its immediate financial obligations without distress. |
| **EPS Growth (5-Year Average)** | `5-Year Average EPS Growth > 5.0%` (indicating consistent growth in earnings per share over the past five years) | Sustainable earnings growth is crucial for long-term value creation. A positive 5-year average EPS growth rate suggests the company is expanding its profitability, even if current valuation metrics are low. |

### How to Apply the Criteria

A company is considered **potentially undervalued** if it meets:

*   **Mandatory:** `P/E > 0` AND `P/B > 0` AND `ROE > 10%`.
*   **At least three (3) of the remaining conditions** from the table above.

### Important Considerations

*   **Industry Context:** Always compare ratios to industry averages and historical trends. Some industries naturally have higher or lower ratios (e.g., tech vs. utilities).
*   **Qualitative Factors:** These quantitative screens are a starting point. Further analysis must include:
    *   Management quality and corporate governance.
    *   Competitive advantages (moats).
    *   Future growth prospects and market positioning.
    *   Industry outlook and macroeconomic conditions.
    *   Specific events or news affecting the company.
*   **Data Quality:** Ensure the financial data used is accurate and up-to-date.
